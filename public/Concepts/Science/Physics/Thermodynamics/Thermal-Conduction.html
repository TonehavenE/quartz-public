    Thermal Conduction

    Feb 07, 20242 min read

    • science/physics

    Topic: Thermodynamics

    Thermal Conduction

    Two materials in contact with one another have a tendency to transfer Heat. On a molecular level, this occurs because the hotter object (or part of an object) has more molecular motion than the cold. When the molecules in the hot air vibrate more quickly, they will collide more often with the cooler ones, transferring energy, which propagates through the medium. Metals are good at this process because of free Electrons, which is also why they are good Electrical Conductors.

    It is experimentally found that the heat flow Q over a time interval t is given by

    tQ​=kAℓT1​−T2​​

    where k is a constant of thermal conductivity (in units J/(s * m * K)), A is the cross-sectional area of the material, ℓ is the thickness of the material, and T1​,T2​ are the temperatures.

    You often see this represented in differential terms, with a temperature gradient:

    dtdQ​=−kAdxdT​

    Thermal Constant

    Materials with high thermal conductivity are said to be good conductors. Those with low values are said to be good insulators.

    R-Values

    Typically measured in Imperial units, a materials R-value measures how good of a insulator it is. R=kℓ​
