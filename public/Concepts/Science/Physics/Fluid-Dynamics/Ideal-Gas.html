    Ideal Gas

    An ideal gas is a gas which obeys the Ideal Gas Law. Specifically, it is a gas which we make certain assumptions about so that we can more accurately and reliably model it using kinetic theory. The assumptions are:

    1. There are a large number of molecules, N, each of mass m, moving in random directions with a variety of speeds
    2. The molecules are, on average, separated by an amount much greater than the diameter of each molecule
    3. Molecules obey the laws of classical mechanics, and interact only in collisions; attractive forces are negligible
    4. Collisions are assumed to be perfectly elastic and of very short duration

    Under these assumptions, we can do some pretty interesting physics to derive a relationship between the kinetic energy and the temperature of a gas.

    Consider a container with a side of area A. It is filled with a gas of N molecules, each mass m. Consider a single molecule of that gas, with an initial velocity v=vx​. It collides with the wall, creating a perfectly elastic collision and bouncing back with a velocity of −vx​. The wall therefore must exert a force of F=dtdP​=dt2mvx​​ on the molecule. The molecule bounces back, hits the other wall, and then returns travelling a total distance of 2ℓ over a time vx​Δt→Δt=vx​2ℓ​. Finally, we can state that the force due to one molecule is

    F=dtdp​=ΔtΔ(mv)​=vx​2ℓ​2mvx​​=ℓmvx2​​

    Now, let us extend to all molecules.

    F=ℓm​(vx12​+vx22​+⋯+vxN2​)

    Let vˉ be the average velocity.

    vx2​ˉ​=Nvx12​+vx22​+⋯+vxN2​​

    We said that the average velocity is in a random direction, so vx​ˉ​=vy​ˉ​=vz​ˉ​. By Pythagoras, we can say that v2ˉ=vx2​ˉ​+vy2​ˉ​+vz2​ˉ​. Therefore, v2ˉ=3vx2​ˉ​.

    F=ℓm​Nvx2​ˉ​=ℓm​N3v2ˉ​

    The pressure on the wall is then P=AF​=31​AℓNmv2ˉ​=31​VNmv2ˉ​→PV=3Nmv2ˉ​=32​N(21​mv2ˉ)

    From the Ideal Gas Law, we know that PV=NkT, so

    32​(21​mv2ˉ)=kT

    The kinetic energy is therefore 23​kT, and thus the average kinetic energy of molecules in an ideal gas is proportional to the absolute temperature of the gas.

    vroot-mean-square​=v2ˉ​=m3kT​​
